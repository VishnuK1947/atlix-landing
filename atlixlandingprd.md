## 1. Context & Goals

Atlix is a real-time narrative intelligence platform — a Bloomberg Terminal for culture and media. The landing page is the first surface most people will encounter (policymaker referrals, investor intros, cold outreach follow-ups), so it has to do two jobs at once:

1. **Lead capture** — convert policymaker / NGO / gov staff visitors into demo requests
2. **Investor signaling** — make it obvious to anyone from In-Q-Tel, Schmidt Futures, or a16z American Dynamism that we are the right team building the right thing

The single biggest piece of feedback from Cheryl's pitch review applies directly here: a visitor must understand **what it is, who it's for, and why now** within 10 seconds of landing on the page. Everything else is downstream of that.

### Success Metrics
- 5%+ visitor → demo request conversion (primary)
- < 10s to comprehension (qualitative — test with 5 non-technical readers)
- Inbound investor inquiries via the page within 30 days of launch
- Time on page > 90s for qualified visitors (proxy for engagement with proof points)

### Non-Goals
- Marketing-team / brand-listening positioning (we are not Brandwatch)
- Live product demo embedded in page (gated behind demo request)
- Pricing transparency (institutional sales motion — pricing on call)
- Blog, content marketing, SEO play (not v1)

---

## 2. Audience

**Primary:** Policymaker offices, gov staff, NGO / think tank research leads. Specifically the chief of staff or legislative assistant tier — the people who triage the daily paper and brief the principal. Per Patrick's feedback, the page must speak in their language (e.g. "field reps," "constituents," "district") and must not read as another marketing-team social listening tool.

**Secondary:** Mission-aligned investors (In-Q-Tel, Schmidt Futures, a16z American Dynamism, Lux, Omidyar). They are reading for thesis fit, team credibility, and category framing — not feature lists.

**Tertiary:** Press, academic partners, prospective hires.

---

## 3. Core Message (the "trunk of the tree")

The page is built around one sentence that must appear, in some form, above the fold:

> **Atlix is real-time narrative intelligence for the people whose job it is to respond — built for policymakers, not marketers.**

Three supporting beats — and we stop at three (per Kirti's "pick a number" note):

1. **What it is** — A narrative intelligence platform. Tracks how political and cultural narratives cluster, spread, and polarize across news and social platforms in real time.
2. **Who it's for** — Policymaker offices, NGOs, and institutional comms teams. Not CMOs.
3. **Why now** — The Jan 6 gap is structural and unsolved. Narratives move at 2026 speed; the institutions that need to respond are reading reports written in 1990. The tools that exist either serve marketers (Brandwatch, Meltwater) or cost $500K+ and require a clearance (Primer, Dataminr). Nothing sits in the middle.

## 4. Page Structure

Single-page scroll. Ordered top to bottom:

### 4.1 Hero
- **Headline:** A sharp restatement of the trunk. Working draft: *"Narrative intelligence for the people who have to respond."*
- **Subhead (1–2 lines):** *"Atlix tracks how political and cultural narratives cluster, spread, and polarize — in real time, across platforms, filtered by geography. Built for policymakers, NGOs, and institutional comms teams."*
- **Primary CTA:** Request a demo
- **Secondary CTA:** For investors → opens contact modal
- **Visual:** A single muted, institutional hero element. Either (a) a stylized geographic heatmap of a narrative spreading across districts, or (b) a clean dashboard screenshot with one narrative thread highlighted. No stock imagery, no abstract gradients. Reference Anduril and Palantir homepages for tone — confident, restrained, technical.

### 4.2 The Problem (the Jan 6 frame)
A single, tight section. No bullet soup. Two short paragraphs:

- **Paragraph 1:** The structural gap. Narratives move from fringe to national in days. The infrastructure built to respond — polling, consulting reports, manual media reading — was designed for a slower world.
- **Paragraph 2:** The Jan 6 example, stated plainly. Signal was visible for months. No system existed to read it. By the time anyone responded, it was too late.

This section is where Cheryl flagged the original pitch as fluffy. Keep it under 120 words total. The example does the work.

### 4.3 Why Hasn't This Been Solved Yet?
Direct answer to the question Cheryl said the pitch must address. A short three-card row (pick a number):

- **Brandwatch / Meltwater** — built for CMOs tracking brand mentions, not for policy teams tracking ideas.
- **Primer / Dataminr** — institutional-grade, but priced for the Pentagon ($100K–$500K+) and procured through cleared channels.
- **McKinsey-style consulting** — six-figure static reports that arrive weeks after the narrative has already moved.

Closing line: *"Atlix sits in the middle. Policy-grade intelligence at NGO-accessible pricing, built native for narrative — not retrofit from a marketing tool."*

This section is doing double duty: it answers the investor question (why now / why hasn't this been built) and the buyer question (why not just use what they already have).

### 4.4 What Atlix Does (3 capabilities)
Three capabilities. Stop at three. Each gets a one-line headline, two lines of body, and a small supporting visual or icon.

1. **Narrative clustering** — See which communities are forming around an idea and how concentrated they are. Not keyword tracking — narrative tracking.
2. **Cross-platform geographic spread** — Watch a narrative move from a regional forum to a national platform, filtered to the district or region you care about.
3. **Polarization index** — Measure whether two sides are hardening or whether the conversation is still diffuse. Real-time, week-over-week.

Per Patrick's feedback: use the term *constituents* and *districts* explicitly in the body copy on this section. The reader should recognize their workflow.

### 4.5 Built for Policy Workflows
Short section addressing the legitimate concerns gov staff raised in feedback. Three lines, framed as principles, not features:

- **An information tool, not a recommendation engine.** Atlix surfaces what's happening. Policy decisions stay with policymakers.
- **Geographic precision.** Narratives filtered by district, region, or country — because national sentiment is not constituent sentiment.
- **Bias-aware.** Online discourse skews. Atlix is transparent about sample composition and provides comparative context, not just raw signal.

This section is critical. It directly addresses Patrick's "listening to social media too much" concern and Barry's sample-bias point. Skipping it costs us credibility with the audience we most need.

### 4.6 Who It's For
Short three-column block. Each column: who they are, what they get out of it.

- **Policymaker offices** — Daily narrative briefings filtered to your district. Supplements field reps, doesn't replace them.
- **NGOs & think tanks** — Track narratives across regions and platforms without standing up your own monitoring team.
- **Institutional comms** — Detect emerging crises early enough to respond, not just to issue statements after the fact.

### 4.7 Backed by / Built at
Credibility row. For v1, what we honestly have:
- USC affiliation
- Any advisor logos we can list with permission (Cheryl, Patrick, Barry, Prof. Jay Wang if he comes on)
- "Currently piloting with [X]" once we have one

If we have nothing yet, omit this section entirely rather than fake it. Empty credibility is worse than no credibility.

### 4.8 CTA Block
Two clear paths, side by side:
- **Request a demo** (primary, gov / NGO / comms audience)
- **For investors** (secondary, opens modal with short blurb + contact email)

### 4.9 Footer
Minimal. Company name, contact email, a one-line mission statement, year. No social links unless we actually post on them.

## 5. Content Requirements

Copy is the hardest part of this page. Drafting principles:

- Every section must pass the "would a chief of staff read this without rolling their eyes" test
- No marketing-speak. No "leverage," "unlock," "empower," "next-generation"
- No buzzword stacks ("AI-powered real-time intelligence platform" → "narrative intelligence, in real time")
- Specifics over adjectives — Jan 6 instead of "high-stakes events"; "filtered to your district" instead of "geographically precise"
- Numbers when honest, omit when not — do not invent traction we don't have

Copy review: at least one read-through with someone who has gov / policy background before launch. Patrick or whoever he connects us with at Prof. Wang's center is the right reviewer.

## 6. Visual & Design Direction

Reference points: **Anduril, Palantir, Scale AI, Rebellion Defense**. Not Linear, not Vercel, not modern-SaaS gradient territory.

- **Palette:** Near-black or deep navy backgrounds, off-white text, one restrained accent color (deep amber or muted institutional blue). No bright purples, no gradients.
- **Typography:** One serious sans (Inter, IBM Plex Sans, or Söhne if budget allows). One monospace for any data or metric callouts. No display fonts.
- **Imagery:** Real product screenshots or stylized data visualizations only. No stock photos, no illustrations of people pointing at laptops.
- **Density:** Closer to a research report than a SaaS landing page. Whitespace generous but not airy. The page should feel serious.
- **Motion:** Minimal. A subtle reveal on scroll is fine. No parallax, no autoplaying video, no animated gradients.

Per the team's earlier feedback to David Han about working with Margaret on UI: the same principle applies here. Design decisions on this page should not be made by the developer alone. Margaret reviews before launch.

## 7. Technical Requirements

Scope is intentionally small — this is a frontend.

- **Stack:** Next.js or plain Vite + React, deployed on Vercel or Netlify. Whatever ships fastest. Reuse the bid-for-aid setup pattern if it accelerates.
- **Forms:** Demo request and investor contact both POST to a simple endpoint. For v1, a single Resend / Loops / Formspree integration emailing the team is fine. No CRM yet.
- **Analytics:** Plausible or Vercel Analytics. We need page views, scroll depth, and CTA click-through. Skip GA.
- **Domain:** atlix.[tld] — confirm what's secured. Reuse jeffreyyang.net DNS knowledge from the earlier bid-for-aid setup if relevant.
- **Performance:** Lighthouse 90+ across the board. This is a static page; there's no excuse not to.
- **Accessibility:** WCAG AA minimum. Real contrast ratios on the dark theme — institutional design often fails this.
- **Responsive:** Desktop-first design (this audience reads on laptops), but mobile must be clean. No broken layouts.

## 9. Risks & Open Questions

- **Bloom positioning.** Cheryl flagged Bloom as a direct competitor with real pilots (VTA, State of California). The landing page needs to read as differentiated from Bloom without naming them. Open question: do we lean harder into the "policy-grade, not civic-engagement" framing to draw a clean line?
- **Sample bias disclosure.** How honest do we get on the page about the limitations of online discourse as a signal? Barry's point was that "supplement" framing kills pricing. The current draft uses "bias-aware" rather than "supplement" — needs gov-side review.
- **Investor vs. buyer voice.** One page serving two audiences is risky. The current structure prioritizes the buyer (policymaker) and lets the investor read between the lines. Worth testing whether that holds, or whether a separate `/for-investors` page is cleaner.
- **Credibility section.** If we genuinely have nothing to put there at launch, the page is weaker. Worth pushing on getting one nameable advisor or pilot before we ship.
